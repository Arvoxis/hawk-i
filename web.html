The Problem — India's Infrastructure Crisis

"India has over 1.5 lakh bridges, thousands of government buildings, hundreds of dams, and crores of square feet of industrial infrastructure — and almost none of it is inspected with any form of intelligence or regularity." Ground reality, 2024

India is in the middle of the largest infrastructure buildout in its history. The government allocated ₹11.11 lakh crore for infrastructure in the 2024–25 budget — roads, bridges, railways, smart cities, hospitals, airports. But here is the problem nobody talks about: we build fast, but we inspect slow — or not at all.

India's National Highway bridges average over 30 years of age. Majerhat bridge in Kolkata collapsed in 2018. The Savitri bridge in Raigad killed 28 in 2016. The Morbi bridge in Gujarat killed 135 in 2022. In every case, the same conclusion in the inquiry: no proper inspection had been done in years.

This is not a budget problem. India spends billions on infrastructure. This is a methodology problem — the inspection process is archaic, manual, dangerous, and completely unscalable.

How inspections happen today in India

Manual rope access
Workers lowered by ropes to inspect bridge undersides. Dangerous, expensive (₹5–15L per audit), covers only accessible sections.
Visual inspection by juniors
PWD engineers walk the surface, tap with hammers, write notes. No quantification. Highly subjective. No GPS tagging.
Inspection frequency
IRC standards say every 5 years. Reality: most bridges go 10–20 years without a structural audit.
No digital record
Findings written in paper registers. No searchable database. No way to track deterioration over time.

Scale of the problem

How ZeroDefect Solves It

The solution in one line
A drone-based AI inspection system where an engineer types "concrete spalling" or "सीमेंट की दरार" into a dashboard — and the drone instantly finds, segments, measures, and reports every instance of it across an entire structure. No training data. No custom models. No risk to human life.

ZeroDefect has three core components working together. The drone acts as the mobile sensor platform — flying autonomous waypoint paths around, beneath, and alongside structures. The AI stack (Grounding DINO + SAM 3 + LLM) turns the camera feed into structured defect intelligence using natural language. The dashboard and report system turns that intelligence into immediately actionable outputs for engineers and government clients.

The key insight is that inspection engineers already know what they're looking for. They don't need the AI to be trained on thousands of images — they need to describe the defect in plain language and have the system find it. That's exactly what Grounding DINO enables. It's a foundation model that was trained on hundreds of millions of image-text pairs, so it already understands concepts like "rusted rebar," "efflorescence," "delaminated concrete," or "corroded cable" — without any fine-tuning.

After detection, SAM 3 draws pixel-precise boundaries around each defect — allowing us to calculate exact crack length in centimetres and damaged area in cm². This is critical for Indian PWD reporting, which requires quantified severity ratings (L1/L2/L3) to trigger repair budgets. The LLM then generates a structured report entry in the format that state PWD departments actually require — in English and optionally Hindi.

Step-by-step: what happens during an inspection flight

1
Mission briefing — engineer defines the inspection
The structural engineer opens the ZeroDefect dashboard on a laptop at the inspection site (could be a PWD office vehicle or a site tent). They upload the structure's boundary polygon on a satellite map, set the flight altitude (typically 5–12m for close inspection), and type the defect queries they want to search for: "cracked concrete . spalling . exposed rebar . rust stain . water seepage marks". The system uploads these as a mission plan to the Pixhawk flight controller via MAVSDK.
QGroundControlMAVSDK-PythonMission Planner
2
Autonomous flight — drone patrols the structure
The quadcopter takes off and executes the pre-planned waypoint mission. It flies in a lawnmower/orbital pattern at the specified altitude, maintaining consistent distance from the structure surface via the FC's altitude hold. The 2-axis gimbal keeps the camera perpendicular to the surface at all times. The Jetson Orin Nano runs the full inference pipeline locally — no internet needed on-site. GPS coordinates are logged at 10Hz via MAVLink. An LTE module (Jio/Airtel 4G SIM) streams annotated video to the dashboard if available; if not, results are cached locally and pushed on landing.
ArduCopter SITLPixhawk 6CHERE3+ RTK GPS4G LTE fallback
3
Edge AI — real-time defect detection on Jetson
Two inference threads run simultaneously on the Jetson Orin Nano Super (67 TOPS). Thread A runs YOLO11n at 60+ FPS via TensorRT INT8 — a fast detector for known defect classes (crack, spalling, rust, missing bolt). Thread B runs Grounding DINO Edge at 8–12 FPS using the engineer's text queries — detecting anything described in natural language regardless of whether it was in any training set. Detections are fused using weighted NMS. Each detection is GPS-tagged by correlating the frame timestamp with MAVLink telemetry.
YOLO11n · TensorRT INT8 · 60fpsGrounding DINO Edge · INT8Weighted NMS fusion
4
Ground station — segmentation and measurement
Detection JSON + compressed frames stream to the ground station laptop via WebSocket over 4G/WiFi. For each high-confidence detection, SAM 3 runs on the laptop's GPU (even an RTX 3060 is sufficient). SAM 3 uses the Grounding DINO bounding box as a prompt and returns a pixel-precise mask of the defect. Using the drone's altitude, GPS, and camera focal length (pre-calibrated), the system converts pixel dimensions to real-world measurements: "Longitudinal crack, length 47cm, width 3.2cm". This data directly maps to IRC 22–2015 severity classification: L1 (minor), L2 (moderate), L3 (critical).
SAM 3 (PyTorch)Pixel→cm conversionIRC 22-2015 severity mapping
5
LLM analysis — structured maintenance report
Every 30 seconds, batched high-confidence detections with annotated images are sent to GPT-4o vision (online) or Gemma-3 12B via Ollama (offline — critical for remote sites in India with poor connectivity). The LLM is given a structured system prompt that matches the PWD inspection report format. Output: severity level, recommended action, urgency timeline, and a plain-language description. The system also generates an optional Hindi summary. All entries populate a live scrolling report panel on the dashboard and feed into the PDF export.
GPT-4o (online)Gemma-3 12B via Ollama (offline)LangChain JSON schema
6
Report generation — PWD-ready PDF in seconds
On mission completion, WeasyPrint generates a PDF structured identically to state PWD bridge inspection formats: cover page (structure name, inspection date, GPS bounds, engineer name), executive summary with overall structural health score (0–100), defect inventory table sorted by severity, GPS map with colour-coded pins, annotated photographs of each defect, and a prioritized action list with estimated repair timelines and budget ranges in INR. This document is immediately submittable to the state PWD — no post-processing needed.

Unique Selling Points

01
Zero-shot detection — no training data ever
Every competing drone inspection product (Skydio, Percepto, Botlink) requires custom ML models trained on thousands of labeled images per defect category. This takes weeks and thousands of dollars per defect type. ZeroDefect uses Grounding DINO — a foundation model — that understands open-vocabulary text. An engineer types the defect, the system finds it. Immediately. For any defect. Forever. This is architecturally impossible with conventional YOLO-based systems.
02
Works offline — built for India's terrain
Most infrastructure in India that needs inspection is in remote locations — NH bridges in the Northeast, dam faces in hill states, power pylons in tribal belts. ZeroDefect runs fully offline: Jetson Orin Nano handles edge inference, Gemma-3 runs locally via Ollama, the dashboard runs on a ₹60K laptop, results save to local PostGIS. No internet required during the mission. 4G is optional for live dashboard streaming in urban sites.
03
Quantified defects, not just photos
Traditional drone inspection gives you a gallery of images. ZeroDefect gives you measurements. SAM 3 pixel masks + GPS altitude = exact crack length in cm, spalling area in cm², water seepage spread in m². These numbers directly map to IRC 22-2015 severity classifications (L1/L2/L3) which determine repair budget allocations in state PWD. The output is a submission-ready PWD report, not a photo album.
04
Temporal tracking — catch deterioration before failure
DINOv2 embeddings from every inspection are stored in PostGIS. When the same structure is re-inspected 3 months later, the system automatically computes a structural change delta — showing exactly which sections deteriorated, by how much, and at what rate. This turns reactive inspection into predictive maintenance — India's infrastructure management goes from "inspect after a collapse scare" to "replace before it fails."
05
Safer than any current alternative
Rope-access inspection workers in India earn ₹800–1200/day with no fall insurance. Several die each year on inspection jobs. ZeroDefect replaces human access to dangerous locations entirely. The drone goes where humans cannot — beneath bridge decks, across dam faces, inside cooling tower voids. Zero human access to hazardous locations during the inspection.
06
10× cheaper than current methods
A standard PWD structural audit by a consulting firm costs ₹5–15 lakh for a medium bridge, takes 2–4 days, and produces a handwritten report. ZeroDefect's SaaS model prices at ₹80K–1.5L per inspection, takes 90 minutes, and delivers a GPS-tagged digital PDF. The cost reduction makes quarterly inspection financially viable for the first time.

3-Day Hackathon Execution Plan

Strategy
Build in layers, not in sequence. The demo-winning core is: drone feed → Grounding DINO detection → boxes appear on dashboard map. Everything else (SAM 3, LLM reports, DINOv2, PDF export) is additive. Finish the core in Day 1. Polish and stack layers in Days 2–3. If hardware runs into issues, pre-captured footage on a laptop can carry the entire demo — judges care about the AI, not the drone flying in front of them.

Target: Drone feed → GDINO detection → map pin appearing live

  • HW3: Assemble and test fly drone (F450 + Pixhawk 6C). Verify ArduCopter stable hover, MAVLink telemetry output.
  • HW1: Export YOLO11n to TensorRT INT8. Verify 60+ FPS on Jetson with sample footage.
